USDA Redesign

This project was an exploration of responsive web design, with a focus on heuristics and accessibility.

Project Overview

Background

Major agencies and initiatives that are household names. like SNAP (Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program) and WIC (Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children), fall under the US Department of Agriculture’s umbrella. It has been called “The People’s Department”, and has impacted the lives of generations of Americans.


Problem

The USDA is a major resource for many user groups, but its confusing navigation and inflated pages make finding critical information difficult and slow. The information itself is focused on what the department has accomplished rather than what services the USDA can provide for the user.


Solution

Simplifying navigation and condensing pages to better address the specific user groups that the USDA serves allowed users to access the information they needed much more smoothly and efficiently.

Users saw a 140% increase in task completion and took 72 seconds less time to complete.

User Research

Proto-Persona

Research was based on the proto-persona of a new mother seeking WIC support from the USDA.

Stephanie wants to apply for WIC benefits which include food packages and education programs. Her goal is to ensure her baby's and her own health, and she is also interested in finding more information on how to manage better nutrition for both of them.

User Path

On the existing USDA website, the shortest path a user can take from the homepage navigates through five different pages to apply to WIC by phone, and seven (including one external) to apply online.

A separate page includes information about the WIC Farmer’s Market Nutrition Program.

Heuristics Analysis

Research was done as a team while the rest of the project was a solo endeavor. After determining the user path, we conducted a heuristics analysis and redlined the pages accordingly. Major issues we noted included no clear hierarchy of information to direct users, pertinent navigation was nested in blocks of text and hard to find, and information was too spread out across multiple user paths.

Usability Testing

We planned and conducted virtual usability tests of the existing USDA website, five different pages to apply to WIC by phone, and three for desktop and two for mobile, and asked potential users to navigate from the homepage to the WIC application page.

We wanted to see how intuitive it felt for users to complete their task and specifically where they got stuck.

“Why isn’t WIC on the homepage?”
“I don’t know where to go, there are too many topics!”
“I think it should be here... but I don’t see it...”
“I give up, I’m just going to use the search bar.”

Affinity Diagram

The team compiled and organized notes from usability testing into an affinity diagram which showed that the users’ experiences overlapped with our own observations from the heuristics analysis. We dot-voted on which issues we thought were the most important to focus on improving and used a prioritization matrix to drive our following iterations.

Define

User Insight

Applicants who need USDA support use the website to search specifically for eligibility and application process information, and want that information to be accessible up-front.



Problem Statement

USDA users want to find relevant information quickly and easily, but the website’s confusing navigation and inflated pages make finding critical information slow and frustrating. The hierarchy of information in both the navigation and page content fails to guide the user’s path.

Ideate

Card Sort

At this point, the research team split up to work on our own redesigns. I went on to conduct a card sort and focused on restructuring the pages so that they could be condensed into the four main categories of USDA support: agriculture and industry, health and nutrition, natural resources, and rural development.

Sitemap

The card sort was turned into a sitemap to help guide the first set of wireframes.

Moodboard

I also created a moodboard at this point to guide the look and feel of the redesign. The redesign still needed to feel approachable and trustworthy to the average American, so natural colors and lifestyle photos as opposed to graphics were emphasized.

Test & Analyze

Usability Testing

Four desktop and two mobile virtual usability tests were conducted with the task of navigating to the WIC program application page from the main USDA homepage in the prototype.

TASK COMPLETION

Overall, users were able to complete the task quickly.

MOBILE MENU

Text was somewhat hard to parse due to its font size and dense content.

DESKTOP MENU

Icons made navigation categories clearer, but letting was too tight between the lines.

2x2 Matrix Analysis

Based on the key findings, I worked on improving menu legibility by increasing both font size and line spacing.

I also added in the desktop version’s icons to the mobile menu to help differentiate categories further.

Iterate

Style Guide

In addition to making the menus more legible, I created a style guide to apply to the hi-fi prototype.

Hi-Fi Prototype

Usability Testing

Ten more usability tests were conducted, half for desktop and half for mobile. Users continued to complete the task quickly and easily.

Minor occasions of confusion were:

HOVER

Some users expected the desktop main menu to appear on hover rather than click.

USER PATH

A couple of users wanted to find the WIC program by first looking at all USDA agencies.

CONTENT

A couple of users didn’t realize there was more content below the fold.

NAVIGATION

There was an interesting divide between those who completely ignored the main menu, and those who ignored the page content.

Lessons Learned & Takeaways

Since this project combined aspects of both UX and UI design, it felt like a much bigger undertaking than previous projects. Graphic design doesn’t come naturally to me, so the UI portion was particularly challenging.

What's Next?

I received many comments over the course of testing about wanting a WIC program button visibly placed on the homepage to make navigation even clearer. However, since the USDA covers many more topics and programs than just WIC, it didn’t make sense to put so much weight on just one program.

As a future step, I would love to show the prototype to someone at the USDA and ask them what their current traffic is like: Is WIC as popular as it seems to be, and does it make sense to give it its own homepage button? I’m also very curious to see if they find the redesign actually useful and if it aligns with the USDA’s goals.
